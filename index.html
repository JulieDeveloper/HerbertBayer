Herbert

Bayer

1900

-1985

An Austrian and American graphic designer, painter, photographer, sculptor, art director, environmental, and interior designer.

He was a leading figure in the Bauhaus movement and is known for his contributions to modern graphic design and typography. Bayer's work often combined geometric shapes, bold colors, and clean, minimalist design principles.

Bayer started as a student at the Bauhaus in 1921, and by 1925, he had become one of the teachers and the leader of the typography and advertisement workshop. His contributions greatly benefited the Bauhaus.

Considering the historical context, Germany's loss in World War I, and the rapid advancements in mass printing technology, Bayer was dissatisfied with the decorated designs of the 1800s and refused to continue the old-fashioned style.

He advocated for transforming the form of visual language to match how people naturally speak. In 1925, he successfully persuaded the Bauhaus leadership to stop using capital letters in the school’s official publications and communication.

This decision was made considering the efficiency and functionality of the typeface. Bayer believed that since spoken words don't include uppercase and it’s quicker to write in German, there should be no need for it in written language.

To demonstrate his idea, Bayer developed a new typography, the "Universal Alphabet", which is characterized by geometric shapes, lowercase letters, and systematic grids. This concept successfully resolved the problems of visual design by combining art and technology. It significantly influenced the development of typography and advertising in Bauhaus.

The evolution of the Universal type demonstrated a shift from nineteenth-century cultural values to an industrial world, where every sector is seeking the best efficiency. This embodies the importance of Bayer's ideas in simplicity and functional design to address societal problems within typography.

Universal Alphabet

In 1925, Bayer designed an experimental typeface named “Universal Alphabet”, which also known as “Universal Typeface”.

He aimed to create designs that were timeless instead of trendy by removing personal style or self-expression from his work. also known as the "Universal Typeface."

This method led Bayer to try to create a typeface with such "basic" letters that would be universally understood by society. By removing the uppercase letter, it would be easier and quicker to write in German. Since most type was produced by machines, Bayer thought it was unnecessary to consider the usage of the chisel. He replaced the touch of the hand with the consistent style of this "rationalized" typeface.

By utilizing a single-case alphabet, which would be easier for children to learn, the lack of uppercase letters would reduce the printer's storage space, setup time, and overall costs. The typeface was designed with machine print and typewriters in mind, further emphasizing its functional aspect while producing efficient results.

Despite its innovative design, the font was not widely used and has been transformed into print. However, it was not meant to be a finalized typeface, but rather a conceptual experiment in developing a visual identity in the alphabet. It played a crucial role in establishing Bayer as a pioneer in functional typography and set the stage for future developments in the field.

Composition Design

The above poster is one of the famous graphic designs created by Herbert Bayer in 1930. The content suggests that it's an advertisement for an exhibition about the Society of Artists and Decorators in Paris.

Analyzing the composition design of the graphic, it's clear that a strong sense of hierarchy is established through the layout structure, font size, and imagery. The layout structure follows a clear grid system, which is very common in modernist design. This grid system helps organize the elements and creates visual order.

The main title is placed at the top, immediately drawing the viewer's attention, followed by the image and the footer text at the bottom. This vertical arrangement guides the reader's eye from top to bottom, effectively communicating the information in a logical sequence.

The design cleverly uses different font sizes to create a hierarchy of information. The title, "allemende", being the most important element, is set in the largest font size. It is followed by the subtitle, set in a smaller font size, which is used for the text "section" and "exposition", and finally, the bottom captions, set in the smallest font size. This gradation in font sizes helps distinguish between the primary, secondary, and tertiary information in the design.

The imagery, which occupies a significant portion of the design, provides a visual representation of the text content. It is placed at the center, balancing the weight of the text above and below. The use of black and white for the graphic shapes also creates a strong contrast with the heading, further emphasizing its importance in the design.

Interestingly, Herbert Bayer's principle in the Universal Alphabet is used throughout the design. This typeface, characterized by its geometric simplicity and lowercase-only principle, gives the design a modern and simple look.
The letterform construction is different from Bayer’s initial design of the Universal Alphabet in 1925. The typeface in the poster appears more narrowed, as you can see in the counter space of the “o”, which has a vertical stress. Also, the apertures, which can be seen in the “a”, “e”, “s”, “g” appear more as a square instead of a circular negative space. The typeface is not constructed by a uniform width. However, it is still a great example for seeing it being used in a composition design, as Bayer’s initial designs were never produced.

Universal Alphabet

Letterform Construction

The Universal Alphabet simplifies Roman letterforms into basic geometric shapes, constructing single-case alphabets using curves and straight lines. Each letterform maintains a consistent stroke weight.

Bayer intended to base his designs on Western letterforms instead of creating a new typeface. When he designed the alphabet "a", he outlined the historical progression of the letter "a", which shows his design referred to the Greek's creation

In the Universal Alphabet, letters with a descender portion, including “g”, “j”, “p”, “q”, and “y”, utilize a short straight line for the part below the baseline. The letters “g” and “q” share the same structure, making them appear similar.

However, they differ by opening up at the top right corner of the bowl. The letter “o” exemplifies the typeface's geometric basis, appearing as a perfect circle in both the counter space and stroke without any uneven stress. Also, the letters “b”, “d”, “p”, and “q” incorporate a circular bowl and perfect circles as the counters, attached to a vertical straight stroke. Also, the letters "c" and "e" share a similar design in their finials, both finishing with the same angle at the end of the apertures.

The letters "m", "n", "w", and "u" feature a perfect half-circle in their shoulder, arc, and vertex, each connected by two straight strokes. The character “v” is formed by two strokes meeting at the bottom, creating a sharply angled crotch. However, its vertex suggests a half-circular outline. This design helps prevent confusion between the letters “v” and “u”.

Bayer removed all unnecessary decoration in the letterform to maintain a clean look. For example, in the letter “f”, he removed the left side of its crossbar, ensuring the width of the crossbar matches almost the same width as its hook. In addition, The Universal Alphabet also showcases unique features. The letter “x” is designed with two half-arched strokes. It is special because it has a different writing sequence than the Roman letter "x". Also, the letter “k” is displayed as a small cap due to the absence of the usual ascender portion.

As a result, the typeface is designed with a strategy in mind. It is a combination of multiple reusable components, which ensures a clean and consistent look by displaying similar parts across the typeface. Despite his intention to design based on Western letterforms, he introduced a new writing sequence in the language. The Universal Alphabet was created without the Roman styling like the serif feature and calligraphic pen trace, it appeared as a simplified, geometric typeface that revolutionized the field of typography.

Influence

Bayer's Universal Typeface has had a remarkable influence on the field of typography. The design concept was groundbreaking at the time, marking a significant departure from the Roman style that characterized the typography of that era. It reflected Bayer's ongoing pursuit of simplicity, functionality, and readability in the field.

Many typographers have designed different versions of typefaces that closely interpret Bayer's Universal Typeface. It led to a new series of redesign typefaces based on Universal Alphabet’s letterform, demonstrating the lasting impact of his work in the field of typography.

ITC Bauhaus

In 1974, The International Typeface Corporation (ITC) redesigned the Universal Alphabet and renamed it “Bauhaus”. Although this version is commonly used, it removed some major concepts from the original drawing, such as geometric figures, simplicity, and a single alphabet.

In Used

The influence of the Universal Alphabet is evident in various design contexts. It is primarily used in large-scale headings and branding logos. Brands like Beats Music, Labello, Dyson, ABC, Pepsi (2014), IHOP, and Pizza Pizza have all incorporated features of the Universal Alphabet into their branding logos.

It's easy to notice that these logos are constructed with perfect circles, such as the letters "b", "d", "p", which consist of a circle at the counter and are attached to a straight stem. Just like the Universal Alphabet, all logos share a universal width.

Although a few of them mix uppercase letters and some letterforms have been altered for branding purposes, it's still noticeable that they reflect the enduring influence of Bayer's revolutionary design.

As the meaning of the typeface changes in different contexts and they are redesigned differently to fit the cultural trend, Bayer’s initial concept and design remain widely influential.

Other Contributiont

Bayer-Type

In 1933, Bayer understood that the real world often preferred serif faces at that time, so he created another typeface for Berthold Type Foundry for commercial use. This typeface, known simply as "Bayer-type.”

While both Universal Typeface and Bayer Type are designed based on geometric shapes, Universal is thought to be “naked”, and Bayer Type is “dressed up”.

It was designed for commercial use, Bayer compromised his lowercase only principle and included capitals, with serif and more decoration added in the letterforms, which appears much stronger contrast than Universal Alphabet.

Although it was never used in the Bauhaus school publication because the school was dissolved after it was published, it had really good reviews and was widely used in the retail market throughout Germany and Scandinavia in the 1930s.

Other Contributiont

Bayer-Shadow

Besides Universal Alphabet and Bayer-Type, Herbert Bayer also designed a unique script without outlines when he was working in Bauhaus.

This script was meant to grab attention in posters and brochures. The aim of this new typeface was to remove national characteristic features in typography. This made international communication easier and more effective.
